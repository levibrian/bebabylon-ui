What is DCA and why it's the smartest thing a regular person can do with their money

You don't need to be a financial expert, watch the news every day, or have a lot of money saved up. Dollar-cost averaging is a simple, proven method that turns small, regular investments into real long-term wealth — and the best part is, you don't even have to think about it.

Let's start with a question

Imagine you want to invest in something — say, a fund that tracks the global stock market. You have two choices:

Option A

Try to pick the right moment

Wait until you think the market is cheap, then put in a big chunk of money all at once. Hope you timed it right.

Option B — DCA

Invest a fixed amount every month, no matter what

Put in €50, €100, or whatever you can afford — automatically — and completely ignore what the market is doing.

Research consistently shows that Option B wins — not just for average people, but even for professional investors. Here's why.

What "dollar-cost averaging" actually means

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) just means: invest the same amount of money at regular intervals, no matter the price.

That's it. No charts. No news headlines. No guessing. You pick a number — say €100 per month — and you invest it on the same day every month, automatically.

Think of it like a gym membership. You pay the same amount every month whether you had a great workout or a terrible one. You don't cancel when it's raining and go twice when the sun is out. You just show up. DCA is the same — you just show up with your €100, rain or shine.

The magic is what happens automatically when you do this. When prices are high, your €100 buys fewer shares. When prices are low, your €100 buys more shares. You end up naturally buying more of something when it's on sale, and less when it's expensive — without having to think about it at all.

A simple example with real numbers

Let's say you invest €50 every month into a stock market fund. Here's what might happen over 6 months:

Month Price per share You invest Shares you get Market mood
January €10.00 €50 5.0 Neutral
February €8.00 €50 6.25 📉 Everyone panicking
March €6.00 €50 8.33 📉 Market still falling
April €7.00 €50 7.14 Recovering
May €9.00 €50 5.56 📈 Going up
June €12.00 €50 4.17 📈 Back above start
Total — €300 36.45 shares —

You invested €300 total and ended up with 36.45 shares. At the June price of €12 each, your investment is now worth €437.40.

That's a €137.40 gain (45.8%) — even though the price only went from €10 to €12 (just 20% up from where you started).

Why did you do better than 20%? Because you bought extra shares when the price dropped to €6 and €8. Those cheap shares are the ones that paid off the most when the market recovered. The dip worked in your favour.

What if you had tried to "time the market" instead?

Say you had waited to invest all €300 in one go. If you managed to buy at the January price of €10, you'd have 30 shares — worth €360 at June's price. That's still a gain, but less than the €437 you made with DCA.

And here's the painful part: most people don't invest at the low. They wait, get nervous, and end up buying when the price is already high — or never buy at all. DCA removes that mistake from the equation entirely.

Why market crashes become your friend

This is the part most people find hard to believe — but it's one of the most important ideas in investing.

When the market drops 20% and the news is full of doom, most people panic and stop investing. But if you're doing DCA, a crash just means your next €100 buys a lot more shares than usual. You're buying things on sale.

Imagine your favourite coffee shop drops their price from €5 to €3 a cup. You'd buy more, right? That's exactly what happens when the stock market drops — except most people do the opposite and run away. DCA keeps you buying when others are running scared.

Over the long term, markets have historically always recovered and gone higher. Every crash in history — 2001, 2008, 2020 — eventually became a recovery. The people who kept investing through those crashes came out ahead.

Why this is perfect for people with a regular salary

DCA was basically designed for how most people actually live. You get paid every month. You set aside a portion — whatever you can afford, even €50. It goes in automatically. You forget about it. You get on with your life.

Why DCA fits your life
  • You don't need a lot to start — €25 or €50 a month makes a real difference over time
  • You don't need to follow the news or watch markets — in fact, it's better if you don't
  • You don't need to make any decisions once you've set it up
  • A market crash isn't a disaster — it's just more shares for your money
  • You get the benefit of compound growth — your gains start generating their own gains

What DCA can't do

It's important to be honest. DCA is powerful, but it's not magic.

It doesn't protect you from a bad investment. If you DCA into one single company and that company goes bankrupt, you still lose. That's why DCA works best with diversified funds — things that own hundreds of companies at once, like an index fund. When one company fails, the others carry on.

It doesn't guarantee profits. No strategy does. But over long periods — 10, 20, 30 years — the odds are strongly in your favour if you're investing in a broad market fund and staying consistent.

You need patience. DCA is a long game. It's not for people hoping to get rich in six months. It's for people who want to be noticeably better off in 10 years than if they'd never started.

How much should you invest?

The honest answer is: whatever you can afford to leave alone. Don't invest money you might need next month for rent or groceries. But anything beyond your emergency fund is fair game.

Even €50 a month adds up. At an average annual return of 7% (roughly what diversified global funds have historically returned), €50 a month turns into roughly €25,000 after 20 years. You'd have put in €12,000 yourself — the market would have added another €13,000.

Start small. Increase the amount as you earn more. The key is to start.

The one thing you have to do

Set it up and don't stop. That's it. The single biggest mistake people make with DCA is panic-stopping when the market drops. That's exactly when you should keep going — because that's when your money buys the most.

The goal is to be boring. Boring investors who invest a fixed amount every month, never check their portfolio during crashes, and just wait — they consistently outperform people who try to be clever about it.

Set up an automatic investment. Pick a date — the 1st of every month. Pick an amount. Pick a diversified fund. Then go live your life and let the math work for you.

The short version
  • DCA means investing the same amount regularly, no matter what the market does
  • When prices fall, you automatically buy more shares — turning crashes into opportunities
  • You don't need to time the market, watch the news, or make any decisions
  • It works best with diversified funds — not single stocks
  • Start with whatever you can afford. Even €50/month compounds into real wealth over time
  • The hardest part is staying consistent. The reward for doing so is enormous
